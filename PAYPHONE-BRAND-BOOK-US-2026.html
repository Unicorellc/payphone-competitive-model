01 — The US Opportunity

And the financial products built for this market are either translations of English-first tools or legacy remittance pipes that treat people as corridors, not customers.

The Underserved Gap

There are two kinds of financial products available to Hispanic and Latino Americans today. Neither one works.

Gap 01

English-First Fintechs That Translate

Venmo, Cash App, and Zelle were built for English-speaking millennials splitting bar tabs. They added Spanish as an afterthought — a language toggle buried in settings. The product architecture, the marketing language, the cultural assumptions, the onboarding flow — all of it was designed for someone else and translated. Translation is not localization. A Spanish-language Venmo is still Venmo. It does not understand that your aunt in Puebla needs her rent money by Friday, not "in 1-3 business days."

Gap 02

Remittance Pipes That Stop at the Border

Remitly, Wise, and Western Union see Hispanic Americans as one end of a corridor. They are optimized for a single transaction: sending money home. They do not help you pay your landlord. They do not help you accept payments for your catering business. They do not give you a card. They are not a financial platform — they are a wire service with an app. The moment the money crosses the border, their relationship with you ends. Payphone's relationship begins.

The Remittance Corridor as Entry Wedge

The United States sends approximately $68 billion in remittances to Latin America every year. That is not a market — it is a river. And rivers have two banks.

Payphone already serves 2 million users on the receiving side of that river in Ecuador. When a user in Houston sends money to their family in Quito using a legacy wire service, the family in Quito may already be a Payphone user. This is the structural advantage no US-born fintech can replicate: Payphone owns the destination.

The entry strategy is simple and powerful: start with the remittance. Own the corridor. Then expand into the sender's full financial life — P2P payments, merchant acceptance, cards, and everything else. The remittance is the door. The financial autonomy platform is the house.

Structural Advantage

"Built in LATAM" is not a liability. It is the proof point. Every US fintech entering LATAM has to learn the market from scratch. Payphone enters the US with millions of users already on the receiving end of cross-border flows. We are not starting from zero — we are completing the circuit.

Five Macro Tailwinds

Tailwind 01

Demographic Inevitability

Hispanic Americans are the youngest major demographic in the US, with a median age of 30 versus 41 for the general population. By 2045, the US will be a minority-majority country. Building for this audience is not a bet on a segment — it is a bet on the future composition of America.

Tailwind 02

Entrepreneurship Surge

Latino-owned businesses are growing at 2x the national rate. There are 4.7 million Hispanic-owned businesses in the US, the majority of them sole proprietorships and micro-businesses — exactly the merchant profile Payphone was built to serve. These are the taqueria owners, the cleaning service operators, the landscapers. They need TAP to Phone. They need payment links. They need zero-cost acceptance.

Tailwind 03

The Bilingual Generation

43 million Americans speak Spanish at home. But the next generation is not Spanish-only or English-only — they are both. They code-switch mid-sentence. They think in two languages. No major fintech has built a product that reflects this reality. Every fintech forces you to choose: English or Spanish. Payphone will be the first to say: both, always, naturally.

Tailwind 04

Cross-Border Commerce Acceleration

Post-pandemic, cross-border e-commerce between the US and LATAM has grown 35% annually. Small businesses on both sides of the border need a single platform to manage payments across currencies and countries. Payphone already operates in this space from the LATAM side. The US launch completes the infrastructure.

Tailwind 05

Regulatory Momentum

US regulators are actively encouraging fintech competition in underserved markets. The CFPB's open banking rules and state-level money transmitter modernization create structural openings for new entrants who serve populations that legacy banks have deprioritized. Payphone enters at the exact moment the regulatory door is widest.

The thesis in one sentence: The US Hispanic market is the size of a G7 economy, growing faster than any other US demographic, entrepreneurially hyperactive, and served by financial products that were either built for someone else or designed to extract a fee from a single transaction. Payphone was built from the ground up for this exact population — just on the other side of the border. Now we cross.

02 — US Brand Positioning

US Brand Positioning

Positioning is the single sentence someone uses to describe you when you are not in the room. In the US market, Payphone needs two positioning statements — one for the people who will use the product, and one for the people who will fund the company.

Consumer-Facing Positioning Statement

For Hispanic and Latino Americans who manage money across two countries, two languages, and two financial systems, Payphone is the financial autonomy platform that unifies your entire financial life — paying, getting paid, sending money home, and running your business — in one app that speaks your language and costs you nothing, unlike legacy fintechs that were built in English and translated, because Payphone was born in Latin America and built for people who live between worlds.

Investor-Facing Positioning Statement

Payphone is the financial autonomy platform for the $2.8 trillion US Hispanic economy — a zero-cost, bilingual-native fintech that combines P2P payments, merchant acceptance (including TAP to Phone), remittance corridors, and card issuance in a single app, entering the US with 2M+ users already on the receiving end of cross-border flows and a proven unit economics model validated across Ecuador and Central America.

The US "Only" Statement

Payphone is the only financial platform in the US market that combines bilingual-native UX, zero-cost P2P and merchant payments, TAP to Phone acceptance, cross-border remittances to LATAM, and card issuance — all in one app, built by a team that already serves 2 million users in the destination countries.

This statement is designed to be tested. If a competitor can truthfully claim all six elements (bilingual-native, zero-cost, TAP to Phone, remittances, card, single app with LATAM presence), the positioning must evolve. As of March 2026, no competitor can.

Category Being Created in the US

The Cross-Border Financial Autonomy Platform

Payphone is not a remittance app. Remittance apps are corridors. Payphone is not a neobank for Hispanics. "Neobank for X demographic" is a slide, not a category. Payphone is not a Spanish Venmo. Venmo in Spanish is still Venmo.

Payphone is a Cross-Border Financial Autonomy Platform: the first complete financial layer designed for people whose money, family, and business exist in more than one country. This category requires four structural elements:

  1. Bilingual-native architecture. Not translation. Not a language toggle. A product that thinks in two languages because its users do. Copy, notifications, support, onboarding — all of it built in both languages from day one, with natural code-switching where appropriate.
  2. Cross-border by default. Every screen, every flow, every feature assumes the user has financial obligations in more than one country. Sending money to Quito is not a special feature — it is as natural as sending money across the street.
  3. Full financial stack. P2P payments, merchant acceptance, cards, links, e-commerce, remittances. One app. One account. No stitching together six apps to manage your financial life.
  4. Zero-cost foundation. The platform monetizes through merchant services and value-added products, never by taxing the individual's transactions. This is not a promotional rate — it is the business model.

03 — US Audience Personas

US Audience Personas

Three personas that represent the core of Payphone's US user base. These are not demographic abstractions — they are psychological portraits built around the specific financial tensions that Hispanic and Latino Americans navigate every day. Each one represents a different relationship with identity, language, and money.

MR
Marco Ramirez
First-Generation Immigrant • 34 • Houston, TX

Archetype: The Provider

Psychology

Marco arrived from Guatemala eight years ago. He works construction and earns $3,200/month, paid in cash or direct deposit depending on the job. He sends $400-600 home to his mother and sister every month without fail. He is deeply financially responsible but largely invisible to the US financial system. He has a bank account at a regional bank — the minimum required to receive direct deposit — but the bank has never offered him a product that reflects his actual financial life. He does not see himself as "underbanked." He sees himself as someone the bank does not care about. He is right.

Pain Points

Remittance fees eat 5-8% of every transfer to Guatemala — that is $240-576/year just to send his own money to his own family. His bank charges $12/month for an account he barely uses. He cannot accept Venmo or Zelle payments from his side jobs because his contacts are split between US and Guatemalan phone networks. He manages his money across three apps (bank, Western Union, WhatsApp for informal tracking) and trusts none of them completely.

Current Solution Stack

Regional bank (grudging necessity), Western Union or Remitly (high cost, high trust), WhatsApp groups for informal lending circles with friends, cash for most daily transactions. He stores savings partly in his US account and partly as cash at home because he does not trust the banking system to not freeze his account over a documentation question.

Trigger Moment

Marco's cousin in Quito tells him about Payphone — the family already uses it there. When Marco discovers he can send money directly to his mother's Payphone account at zero cost and she receives it instantly, the math changes overnight. He saves $400+ per year on remittance fees alone. That is a month of rent for his mother. The conversion is not emotional — it is arithmetic. Then he discovers he can also use the same app for P2P payments with his crew, accept payments for side jobs, and get a card. One app replaces four.

Emotional Conversion Need

Marco needs to feel respected, not rescued. He is not a charity case. He is a provider who works harder than most people and sends money home because that is what family means. Payphone's brand must never position itself as "helping the underserved." It must position itself as the tool that finally matches the sophistication of Marco's actual financial life — a life that crosses borders, currencies, and languages every single day.

"I send money to my mother every two weeks. Western Union charges me $12 each time and the exchange rate is never what they say it is. I am paying $600 a year for the privilege of supporting my own family. Find me a better way and I will switch tonight."
SV
Sofia Vargas
Bicultural Millennial • 28 • Miami, FL

Archetype: The Bridge

Psychology

Sofia was born in Miami to Colombian parents. She speaks perfect English and perfect Spanish and switches between them mid-conversation without thinking about it. She has a marketing job at a tech company ($72K/year), shops at Trader Joe's, listens to Bad Bunny and Billie Eilish in the same playlist, and watches both the Super Bowl and Copa America. She is fully American and fully Latina — she rejects the idea that she has to choose. She uses Venmo for splitting dinners and Zelle for rent, but neither app reflects the full picture of her financial identity. She occasionally sends money to her grandmother in Bogota through her parents, but she has never done it directly because the process feels like it was designed for someone older.

Pain Points

No financial app speaks to her the way she actually lives. Venmo feels aggressively American. Remittance apps feel like they are built for her parents' generation — clunky, transactional, zero personality. She wants a financial product that reflects her bicultural identity without making it a novelty. She is tired of being a "Hispanic consumer segment" in someone's marketing deck. She wants a product that just gets it without labeling it.

Current Solution Stack

Venmo (social payments, peer-to-peer), Zelle (rent and formal payments), Apple Pay (in-store), Robinhood (investing, occasional), her parents' Remitly account (when grandmother needs something). She has considered starting a side business selling curated Latin American home goods online but has not found a payment platform that handles both US and international transactions without feeling like an enterprise tool.

Trigger Moment

Sofia sees a Payphone ad on Instagram that is in English but drops a Spanish word naturally — the way she talks. It does not feel like a translation. It does not feel like pandering. It feels like someone made a financial app for people who live the way she lives. She downloads it out of curiosity. When she sees the interface shift between English and Spanish based on context — a payment confirmation to her friend in English, a transfer to Bogota with her grandmother's name rendered correctly with the accent marks — she feels something she has never felt from a fintech app: recognition.

Emotional Conversion Need

Sofia needs to feel seen, not targeted. The difference is everything. Targeting is what banks do when they put a salsa graphic on a checking account ad in February. Being seen is building a product that natively handles the accent mark in her grandmother's name. Payphone's brand must signal cultural fluency, not cultural marketing.

"I do not need a 'Latino fintech.' I need a fintech that was built by people who understand that my life does not fit into one country, one language, or one culture. And I can tell in three seconds if a brand actually gets that or is just performing it."

A client wants to pay for a $3,800 backyard project with a card. Roberto's Square reader is dead (no charge). He is about to lose the immediate payment and chase an invoice. He downloads Payphone, onboards in two minutes, and accepts the $3,800 payment on the spot. At zero cost instead of Square's $99.80 processing fee. That single transaction pays for the mental switch. Roberto needs to feel like a professional, not a micro-business being managed. He built this company from nothing. He employs people. He pays taxes. He wants a tool that treats him like the $250K/year business he is, not like a side hustle. Payphone's brand must communicate: you built something real. Here are the tools that match.

"I pay Square almost $6,000 a year in fees. Six thousand dollars. For what? For a little white box that runs out of battery at the worst possible time? My phone never runs out of battery. Why can I not just use my phone?"

What Changes for the US

Bilingual Strategy: The Three Registers

Payphone in the US does not operate in "English" or "Spanish." It operates in three linguistic registers, and the choice of register is determined by context, audience, and intent.

Register When to Use Example
English-Primary Investor materials, US press, App Store listing, LinkedIn, general market ads "Your phone is your entire financial infrastructure."
Spanish-Primary Remittance-focused messaging, first-gen immigrant outreach, WhatsApp/community content, in-app when user language is Spanish "Tu dinero llega al instante. Sin costo. Sin complicaciones."
Code-Switch Social media, brand campaigns targeting bicultural audiences, organic content, Instagram/TikTok "Send money home in seconds. Porque la familia no puede esperar 3-5 business days."

Code-Switching Rules

Code-switching is the most powerful tool in Payphone's US linguistic arsenal. Done well, it creates instant recognition and cultural intimacy. Done badly, it reads as pandering. These rules prevent the bad version:

Rules for Code-Switching

01. The switch must happen where a bilingual person would naturally switch. This is the non-negotiable rule. If you would not say it that way in a conversation with a bilingual friend, do not write it. Every code-switch should pass the "dinner table test" — would this sound natural at a family dinner where both languages are spoken?

02. The Spanish portion must carry meaning, not decoration. Never insert a Spanish word just to signal "we speak Spanish." The Spanish element should deliver the emotional punch, the cultural insight, or the specific reference that English cannot carry as well.

03. Both languages must be grammatically correct in their own right. Code-switching is not broken English or broken Spanish. It is two correct languages flowing together. Sloppy grammar in either language destroys credibility instantly.

04. Never translate the code-switch. If you write a line that mixes English and Spanish, do not follow it with a parenthetical translation. The reader either gets it or they are not the target audience for that particular piece. Explaining the switch kills the intimacy that made it powerful.

05. Limit to one switch per unit of copy. A social post gets one code-switch. A headline gets one. An email gets one or two. More than that reads as a gimmick, not a voice.

Never Do This

"Hola amigos! Ready to send dinero to your familia? Payphone makes it muy facil!" — This is not code-switching. This is a costume. It inserts random Spanish words into English syntax for decoration. A bilingual person would never speak this way. It reads as a brand that Googled "Spanish words for marketing" and scattered them like confetti.

Tone by US Context

Tone: Warm + Direct. Remittances are not transactions — they are acts of love with a dollar sign. The tone acknowledges the emotional weight without being sentimental. Lead with the outcome (your family receives money), not the process (our platform transfers funds).

Tone: Culturally fluent + Sharp. This is where code-switching lives. The brand shows it understands the audience not by saying so, but by speaking the way they speak.

"Your money crosses borders every month. Your financial app should have been built to do the same."

05 — US Visual Identity Adaptations

US Visual Identity Adaptations

Payphone's visual identity was built to scale globally. The color system, typography, logo, and design principles do not change for the US. What adapts is the imagery, the cultural context of the visuals, and the competitive visual environment the brand must differentiate within.

What Stays the Same (Non-Negotiable)

Sacred

The Color System

Naranja Payphone (#FF6400), Violeta (#5A00B4), Amarillo (#FFA000), Oscuro (#222222), Blanco (#FFFFFF). All gradients (Sunset, Electric, Dawn). These are the DNA. They do not localize. An orange Payphone screen in Houston must be the same orange as in Quito. Brand recognition depends on this consistency across markets.

Sacred

The Whale Tail Logo

Same mark, same rules, same clear zones, same color applications. The whale tail does not need a US adaptation — whales cross oceans. That is the metaphor. If anything, the logo becomes more resonant in the US context: a brand that migrated from Latin America, just like its users.

Sacred

Typography: Aeonik

Same typeface, same weights, same scale, same spacing rules. Aeonik reads well in both English and Spanish without special character rendering issues. Its geometric warmth translates across languages and cultural contexts.

Sacred

Design Principles

Premium minimalism, generous white space, three-size-maximum typography rule, warm color grading, sentence case headlines. These are brand constants. The US team does not get to add drop shadows or gradients-on-text because "the US market expects it." The brand's restraint is its competitive advantage in a market full of visual noise.

What Adapts for the US

Imagery: US-Specific Contexts

The three-word image filter (Authentic, Dynamic, Warm) remains. But the subjects and settings shift to reflect the US audience and geography. Every image must still pass the filter — no exceptions for market adaptation.

  5. The American Dream reimagined — not the cliche (white picket fence) but the real one: a woman accepting her first card payment at her own business, a father checking his phone after a successful remittance, a couple reviewing their combined finances in one app.

06 — US Go-to-Market Brand Strategy

US Go-to-Market
Brand Strategy

The brand enters the US not with a launch event but with a movement. The strategy is built on the principle that Payphone's best marketing asset is the story it already has: 2 million users, built in the middle of the world, now crossing the border just like its audience did.

The Launch Narrative

"We did not build Payphone in Silicon Valley and bring it to Latin America. We built it in the middle of the world — Ecuador, la Mitad del Mundo — where a vendor losing a sale because she could not accept a card was not a market research finding but something we saw every morning on the way to the office. Two million people later, we are bringing it to the United States. Not because the US needs another fintech. Because 63 million Hispanic Americans deserve a financial platform that was built by people who understand what it means to manage money across two countries, two languages, and two lives."

This narrative does three things simultaneously: it establishes credibility (2M users), it creates emotional resonance (built where you come from), and it differentiates structurally (this is not a Silicon Valley product adapted for Hispanics — it is a LATAM product that naturally expands to the US Hispanic market).

PR Angle: The Reverse Migration Story

Every LATAM fintech story in US media follows the same arc: "US-backed fintech expands to Latin America." Payphone's story is the opposite, and that inversion is the PR hook.

Headline Angle 01

The LATAM Fintech That Came North

"While US fintechs are racing to 'expand to LATAM,' this Ecuadorian fintech with 2M users is doing the reverse — entering the US to serve the 63M Hispanic Americans that Venmo and Cash App never built for." This angle pitches to TechCrunch, The Information, Bloomberg Linea, Rest of World.

Headline Angle 02

The $68 Billion Fee Nobody Talks About

"Hispanic Americans pay $4-5 billion per year in remittance fees alone — a tax on family love. This fintech plans to make it zero." This angle pitches to consumer press: CNBC, Forbes, NPR's Latino USA, the Wall Street Journal's personal finance section.

Headline Angle 03

TAP to Phone Is About to Kill the POS Terminal

"4.7 million Latino-owned businesses in the US. Most of them pay processing fees they cannot afford, on hardware they do not need. One fintech just made the terminal extinct." This angle pitches to payments press: PYMNTS, The Fintech Times, Payments Dive, American Banker.

Influencer and Community Strategy

Payphone does not buy influencers. It recruits cultural allies — people whose authentic lives demonstrate Payphone's value without a script.

Tier 1: Founder-Led Content (Juan Diego / Juanjo)

Juan Diego's personal brand is the primary distribution channel for the US launch narrative. The focus shifts from LATAM fintech thought leadership to the US expansion story — a founder who built for 2M users in Ecuador, now building for 63M in the US. Platform: LinkedIn (primary), X (secondary), podcast guest circuit.

Tier 2: Cultural Amplifiers (10-15 Voices)

Not influencers — amplifiers. These are real people with engaged audiences in the US Latino space who can speak to Payphone's value from personal experience.

09 — US Brand Rules

US Brand Rules

Ten prohibitions specific to the US market. These exist because the cultural, competitive, and regulatory environment in the US creates unique traps that well-intentioned marketers fall into every day. These walls prevent Payphone from becoming "just another Hispanic fintech."

01
Never say "for the underbanked." Payphone does not serve "underbanked" people. That is a term invented by banks to describe their own failure to serve a market. Payphone serves people whose financial sophistication exceeds the products available to them. The framing is "better tools for sophisticated financial lives" — not "access for the underserved." One positions the user as capable. The other positions the user as lacking.
02
Never use stock photography of "Hispanic families." Every stock photo agency has a folder called "Hispanic Lifestyle" filled with images of multi-generational families laughing around a kitchen table. These images are not representation — they are decoration. Payphone uses real photography of real people in real environments. If the image could appear on a Remitly ad, a Wells Fargo branch poster, and a Goya Foods commercial simultaneously, it is too generic to use.
03
Never scatter random Spanish words into English copy. "Hola amigos, ready for some easy dinero?" is not bilingual marketing. It is a costume. Code-switching follows the rules defined in Section 04: the switch must happen where a bilingual person would naturally switch, the Spanish must carry meaning (not decoration), and both languages must be grammatically correct. When in doubt, write the entire piece in one language rather than doing the code-switch badly.
04
Never use the word "community" as a substitute for specificity. "We serve the Hispanic community" means nothing because "the Hispanic community" is not one thing. It is 63 million people from 20+ countries with different dialects, cultures, immigration histories, and financial needs. Always be specific: "We serve Latino small business owners in Houston" or "We serve first-generation immigrants who send remittances to Central America." Specificity is respect. Generalization is laziness.
05
Never position Payphone as a charity or a social mission. Payphone is a business. A good business that does right by its users, but a business. The zero-cost model works because of structural unit economics, not because of altruism. Positioning the company as a social enterprise invites lower valuations, mission-drift expectations, and the assumption that profitability is a secondary concern. It is not. Payphone makes money by saving people money. That is capitalism at its most aligned.
06
Never reference immigration status, documentation, or legal residency in marketing. Payphone serves everyone who qualifies under US financial regulations. The brand never references, implies, or markets around immigration status. This is both an ethical commitment and a regulatory requirement. The product speaks to financial needs, never to legal categories. Any content that could be interpreted as targeting undocumented populations — even with good intentions — is a compliance and brand risk that must be avoided absolutely.
07
Never assume all Hispanic Americans speak Spanish. One in four US Hispanics speaks English only. The bilingual strategy is an asset, not a requirement for the user. Payphone works perfectly in English-only mode. The Spanish capability is there when users want it — it is never forced. Marketing materials should reflect this: lead in English, offer Spanish as a natural option, never require it.
08
Never compare Payphone to legacy remittance services by mocking them. Western Union may charge high fees, but for millions of people, it was the lifeline that kept families connected. Many Payphone users have deep emotional associations with these services — they remember their mother walking to a Western Union office. Payphone competes on product superiority, not by belittling the tools that served people when nothing else would. Critique the model. Respect the history.
09
Never use flags, national colors, or country-specific iconography in general marketing. The US Hispanic market includes people from Mexico, Guatemala, El Salvador, Colombia, Ecuador, Dominican Republic, Cuba, Puerto Rico, Peru, and more. A Mexican flag does not represent a Guatemalan. A Colombian coffee graphic does not represent an Ecuadorian. General market materials use Payphone's own brand identity — never borrowed national symbols. Country-specific imagery is permitted only in corridor-specific campaigns targeting a single nationality.
10
Never dodge the name question — own it. "What is a Payphone?" will be asked. Frequently. By Americans who associate the word with the extinct street-corner telephone. Do not avoid this. Lean into it. The answer is the brand story: "A payphone was the first piece of democratic communication technology — anyone could use it, the barrier was a coin, and the technology disappeared behind the connection. We named our company after that principle. Anyone can use Payphone. The barrier is zero. And the technology disappears behind the thing that matters: your money, your family, your business." The name's apparent obsolescence is actually its most memorable quality. It generates the question that gives us the stage to deliver the answer.

Cultural Sensitivity Framework

Before any US-market content ships, it must pass this three-question filter:

Question 01

"Would a first-generation immigrant from Guatemala and a third-generation Mexican-American in LA both feel respected by this content?" If the content works for one but alienates the other, it is too narrow. Payphone's US brand must bridge generational and national differences without erasing them.

Question 02

"Does this content reduce a person to their ethnicity, or does it speak to a financial need that their ethnicity happens to inform?" Payphone is a financial product, not a cultural product. The bilingual, cross-border features exist because of real financial needs — not because "Hispanics like things in Spanish." Functionality first, cultural resonance second.

Question 03

"Would this content work if Payphone's name and logo were removed?" If the answer is yes — if any fintech could have made this ad — it is too generic. Payphone's content must be ownable. Only Payphone has the LATAM origin story, the 2M-user proof point, the TAP to Phone differentiator, and the cross-border architecture. Content that does not leverage at least one of these is wasting Payphone's competitive position.
